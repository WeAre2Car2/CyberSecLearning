The big idea: your “AI-resistant” cybersecurity profile
Aim to become this kind of beginner over 12–18 months:
Technically dangerous in labs, legally safe in real life: web attacks, privilege escalation, recon, basic AD, cloud/IAM misconfigurations — only in sanctioned labs. [portswigger.net], [portswigger.net], [academy.ha...thebox.com]
Defensively literate: logs, detections, incident basics, hardening, Zero Trust, threat modeling. [learn.microsoft.com], [academy.ha...thebox.com]

Comfortable with AI as a tool and attack surface: use AI to speed up learning/reporting, but also learn where AI systems fail. Recommended career direction for you
For a beginner who likes offense, the best starter specialization is:
1) Start with web app security
Why:
It’s the most accessible offensive path for a beginner.
You can build and break your own apps safely.
It teaches HTTP, auth, sessions, APIs, input handling, and business logic.
PortSwigger’s Web Security Academy is free, legal, hands-on, and designed exactly for this style of learning. [portswigger.net], [portswigger.net]
2) Then add internal/Windows/Active Directory
Why:
AD is still central in enterprise environments, and HTB emphasizes that it is present in the majority of corporate environments and is a major attack surface. [academy.ha...thebox.com]
3) Then add cloud + identity + detection
Why:
This is where AI-resistant value grows. People who can connect offensive findings to cloud IAM, identity abuse, hardening, detections, and Zero Trust design are much rarer than people who can just run scanners. WEF expects demand for networks and cybersecurity plus durable skills like resilience and analytical thinking to rise, and Microsoft’s Zero Trust guidance makes identity and least privilege core modern security concepts. [weforum.org], [learn.microsoft.com]
So the path is:
Web Offense → Internal/AD → Cloud/Identity/Detection → AI Security + Reporting

Use AI for:

brainstorming hypotheses

Do not use AI to:

reason through ambiguity
connect systems
judge risk
explain tradeoffs

That matches both WEF’s emphasis on analytical thinking/resilience and ISC2’s finding that employers increasingly prioritize problem-solving and communication in an AI-shaped workforce. [weforum.org], [xmc-qa-ver...l.isc2.org], [isc2.org]

My blunt advice: what not to do
Avoid these beginner traps:
Trap 1: Tool collecting
Knowing names of tools is not skill.
Trap 2: CTF-only thinking
Great for learning, weak if you never connect it to real systems and reporting.
Trap 3: Offense-only ego
The durable pros understand defense, architecture, and remediation.
Trap 4: Tutorial addiction
If you don’t build notes, scripts, reports, and projects, you’re not compounding.
Trap 5: Illegal curiosity
Keep everything to labs, self-hosted projects, and authorized environments only.
PortSwigger explicitly frames its academy as a safe and legal way to learn hacking, and that’s the right model. [portswigger.net]
